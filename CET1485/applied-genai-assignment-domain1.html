Part 2 · The Seven Prompt Engineering Techniques

Prompt engineering is the practice of designing your inputs to get useful, accurate outputs from a Gen AI tool. The same question phrased differently can produce dramatically different responses. The techniques below are the established core toolkit — you will apply them throughout this assignment and name them in your deliverable.

TECHNIQUE 1

Zero-Shot Prompting

What it is: Ask a direct question with no examples and no extra context. The AI uses only its training to respond.

When to use: Quick factual lookups, simple definitions, common knowledge. Fastest, but most prone to hallucination on specific facts.

Example — zero-shot
What is the difference between TCP and UDP?
TECHNIQUE 2

Few-Shot Prompting

What it is: Provide 1-3 examples of the pattern you want the AI to follow before asking your real question. The AI learns the format from your examples.

When to use: When you want output in a very specific format (flashcards, table rows, exam questions, mnemonics) and a single description isn't enough.

Example — few-shot
Create flashcards for Network+ ports in this exact format:

EXAMPLE 1:
Front: What port does HTTPS use?
Back: TCP 443 — encrypted web traffic.

EXAMPLE 2:
Front: What port does SSH use?
Back: TCP 22 — secure remote shell.

Now create 5 more flashcards for: DNS, DHCP, SMTP, SNMP, NTP.
TECHNIQUE 3

Role / Persona Prompting

What it is: Tell the AI who it should be when answering. "You are a..." sets context and shifts vocabulary, tone, and depth.

When to use: When the audience or expertise level matters. Different personas yield different outputs for the same underlying question.

Example — role prompting
You are a senior network engineer with 15 years of enterprise
experience. A junior technician asks you why their firewall is
dropping VoIP calls during nightly backups.

Explain the likely cause and what to check first, in the
no-nonsense tone you'd actually use with a junior on your team.
Avoid textbook definitions — focus on what to DO.
TECHNIQUE 4

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Prompting

What it is: Ask the AI to show its reasoning step by step before giving the final answer. The phrase "think step by step" or "show your work" triggers this.

When to use: Subnetting calculations, troubleshooting logic, multi-step scenarios. Better accuracy on complex problems because the AI exposes the path to its answer (and you can spot errors mid-stream).

Example — chain-of-thought
Given the IP address 192.168.50.130 with subnet mask /27,
calculate the network address, broadcast address, first usable
host, and last usable host.

Think step by step. Show your work at each stage:
1. Convert /27 to dotted decimal mask
2. Determine the block size
3. Find the network address
4. Find the broadcast address
5. List the first and last usable hosts

Then summarize the answer.
TECHNIQUE 5

Structured Prompting

What it is: Break your prompt into labeled sections — CONTEXT, TASK, CONSTRAINTS, FORMAT — so the AI knows exactly what each piece of information is for.

When to use: Complex requests with multiple requirements. Highly effective when you want consistent output structure or when constraints matter (length limits, required sections, banned terminology).

Example — structured prompt
CONTEXT: I'm preparing for the CompTIA Network+ N10-009 exam.
I have 2 weeks until my test date. I'm strongest on cabling
and weakest on cloud concepts.

TASK: Create a 14-day study plan focused on my weak area.

CONSTRAINTS:
- Maximum 1 hour per day
- Include both reading and practice questions
- Build in 1 review day every 3 days
- No paid resources

FORMAT: Table with columns: Day, Topic, Activity, Time, Resource.
TECHNIQUE 6

Iterative Refinement

What it is: Multi-turn conversation. You don't accept the first answer — you push back, request changes, add constraints, and refine until the output is right. Each turn builds on prior context.

When to use: Almost everything serious. The first AI response is rarely the best one. Power users don't write better single prompts — they have more productive conversations.

Example — iteration prompts
Turn 1: "Explain VXLAN to me."

Turn 2: "That was too generic. Focus specifically on why VXLAN
exists — what problem did it solve that VLANs couldn't?"

Turn 3: "Now give me a 3-bullet summary I could use as a flashcard."

Turn 4: "Replace the second bullet — it uses too much jargon.
Rewrite for a Net+ student who hasn't covered data center yet."
TECHNIQUE 7

Self-Critique / Verification Prompting

What it is: After the AI gives an answer, prompt it to review its own work for errors, contradictions, or weak spots. Sometimes catches mistakes — but unreliable on its own. Always combined with human verification.

When to use: After getting any answer involving specific facts, calculations, or claims. A useful first-pass error check, but not a substitute for authoritative sources.

Example — self-critique
Review your previous answer about NTP and SNMP port numbers.

For each protocol you mentioned, check:
- Is the port number correct?
- Is the transport protocol (TCP or UDP) correct?
- Are there any common variations or "well-known wrong" answers
  I should be aware of?

If you find any errors in your earlier response, correct them
explicitly and state what you changed.
★ COMBINE TECHNIQUES

Real prompt engineering rarely uses one technique in isolation. A polished prompt often combines role + structured + chain-of-thought all at once. Example: "You are a Net+ instructor (role). Given this scenario (context), explain step by step (CoT) using this format (structure)..."

    Where Copilot commonly gets networking wrong

    Modern AI tools — Copilot included — hallucinate confidently on networking specifics. The table below shows real failure modes documented in early 2025-2026 evaluations. None of these are theoretical — each has been observed in actual AI output. Use this as your watch list while completing the assignment.

    Topic AreaCommon AI ErrorAuthoritative Answer
    Port numbers "NTP uses TCP 123" or "SNMP uses TCP 161" Both use UDP. UDP 123 (NTP) and UDP 161 (SNMP). AI conflates with TCP because most port references are TCP.
    Port numbers "SMTP submission uses port 25 with TLS" Modern SMTP submission uses port 587 (STARTTLS) or 465 (SMTPS). Port 25 is server-to-server transfer.
    OSI Model "TLS encryption operates at Layer 4" TLS is commonly described as Layer 6 (Presentation), though it runs above Layer 4 transport (TCP).
    Fiber distances "OM3 multimode reaches 1 km at 10G" OM3 ≈ 300 m at 10GBASE-SR. OM4 ≈ 400 m. Single-mode (10GBASE-LR) reaches 10 km.
    Wi-Fi standards "Wi-Fi 6 = 802.11ac" or "Wi-Fi 5 = 802.11ax" Wi-Fi 5 = 802.11ac. Wi-Fi 6 = 802.11ax. Wi-Fi 6E = 802.11ax + 6 GHz. Wi-Fi 7 = 802.11be.
    IaaS vs PaaS "AWS EC2 is PaaS because it runs in the cloud" EC2 is IaaS (raw VMs you manage). PaaS = Heroku, App Engine, Elastic Beanstalk.
    Scalability vs Elasticity Used interchangeably as if they mean the same Scalability = ability to grow. Elasticity = automatic up AND down with demand.
    IDS vs IPS "IDS blocks attacks at the firewall" IDS detects only. IPS sits inline and blocks. D = Detect, P = Prevent.
    IPv4 subnetting "/24 has 256 usable hosts" /24 has 256 total but only 254 usable (subtract network and broadcast).
    NAT "NAT encrypts traffic between networks" NAT translates addresses; it does NOT encrypt. VPN does the encryption.
    Your 4-Step Verification Workflow
    STEP 1

    Notice specificity

    When Copilot gives a specific number, date, RFC, or distance, that's exactly when to verify. General statements are usually safer than specific ones — hallucinations cluster around precise facts.

    STEP 2

    Cross-reference against the CompTIA objectives

    The N10-009 official exam objectives PDF (free at comptia.org) is the source of truth for what the exam tests. If Copilot claims something the objectives contradict, trust the objectives.

    STEP 3

    Use authoritative sources for technical facts

    Port numbers: IANA Service Name and Transport Protocol Port Number Registry. RFCs: rfc-editor.org. Fiber specs: IEEE 802.3. Wi-Fi: Wi-Fi Alliance website. Copilot is not these sources — it tries to remember what they say.

    STEP 4

    Use self-critique prompting, but don't rely on it

    Asking Copilot to review its own work (Technique 7) sometimes catches errors — but a hallucinating model can confidently confirm its own wrong answer. Self-critique is a useful first pass, never the last word.
