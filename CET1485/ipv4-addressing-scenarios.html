RFC 1918 Private Ranges

Defined by RFC 1918. Used inside organizations, NOT routable on the public internet. Three ranges, one per legacy class:

  • 10.0.0.0/8 — 10.0.0.0 to 10.255.255.255 (16.7M addresses)
  • 172.16.0.0/12 — 172.16.0.0 to 172.31.255.255 (1M addresses)
  • 192.168.0.0/16 — 192.168.0.0 to 192.168.255.255 (65K addresses)

Watch the 172 range — only 172.16–172.31 are private. 172.15.x and 172.32.x are public.

APIPA — 169.254.0.0/16

Automatic Private IP Addressing. When a host can't reach a DHCP server, Windows (and most modern OSes) self-assign an address in 169.254.0.1 – 169.254.255.254.

APIPA addresses can only reach other APIPA-addressed hosts on the same Layer 2 segment. They cannot reach the internet or gateway.

If a user reports they can't reach anything and ipconfig shows a 169.254.x.x address → DHCP is the problem.

Loopback — 127.0.0.0/8

The loopback range refers to the local host itself. Almost always written as 127.0.0.1 ("localhost"), but the entire 127.0.0.0/8 range is reserved.

Pinging 127.0.0.1 tests the TCP/IP stack on the local machine — packets never leave the OS. If ping 127.0.0.1 fails, the IP stack itself is broken.

Class-A gotcha: 127 is technically in the Class A first-octet range (1–126 + 127), but it's carved out as loopback and is NEVER used as a regular host address.

CIDR & VLSM

CIDR (Classless Inter-Domain Routing) replaces classful boundaries. 192.168.5.0/24 means "first 24 bits are network." The /N is the prefix length.

VLSM (Variable Length Subnet Mask) divides one block into subnets of different sizes. A /24 can be split into a /26 for 60 hosts AND a /27 for 30 hosts AND a /28 for 14 hosts — efficient use of addresses.

Classless means routes are advertised with their actual mask, allowing tighter aggregation and smaller routing tables.
