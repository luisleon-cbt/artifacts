A network's topology is its physical and logical wiring pattern — how the boxes connect to each other. Different shapes serve different goals: maximum redundancy, minimum cost, predictable latency, easy troubleshooting. The exam expects you to recognize each topology by its diagram, know what it's good and bad at, and pick the right one for a given scenario. This drill walks all five topologies, both architectures (three-tier and collapsed core), and the two traffic flows that data centers care about most.

Three-Tier Hierarchical

The classic enterprise design. Three distinct layers, each with a clear role. Used in larger campuses, multi-floor offices, and traditional data centers.

The Three Layers

Core High-speed backbone — moves traffic between distribution blocks. Optimized for raw throughput. No filtering, no policy.
Distribution Aggregation point — connects access switches to the core. Handles routing, ACLs, QoS, redundancy.
Access End-host connectivity — where PCs, phones, APs, printers plug in. Where VLANs are typically assigned.

When to Use

  • Large campuses with thousands of endpoints across multiple buildings
  • Strict separation of routing and switching responsibilities
  • Highest scalability — each layer can be expanded independently
Collapsed Core (Two-Tier)

The three-tier model with the core and distribution layers merged into a single layer. Reduces device count, hops, cost, and complexity at the price of less raw scale.

The Two Layers

Core+Dist Combined layer — handles backbone and aggregation/routing duties together. Often a pair of L3 switches at the top.
Access Same role as in three-tier — end-host connectivity, VLAN assignment.

When to Use

  • Small to mid-size networks — single building, modest user count
  • Cost sensitivity — fewer devices, lower licensing, simpler ops
  • Faster east-west — fewer hops between distribution and core
