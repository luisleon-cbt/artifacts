The complete reference.

A networking closet — small enough to be a wall-mount cabinet, large enough to be a dedicated data center — is governed by four constraints: space (rack U), power (watts and amperage), cooling (temperature), and physical security. Get any one wrong and the network doesn't run reliably. This tab covers each in turn.

MDF vs IDF
MDF

Main Distribution Frame

The central hub of a building's network. One per building. Houses the core router, primary switches, demarc to ISP, and main patch panels. Often combined with the server room or main data center.

RULE: One MDF per building. Cabling from MDF reaches IDFs via vertical risers.

IDF

Intermediate Distribution Frame

A floor-level wiring closet with access switches that connect to end-user devices on that floor. Uplinked to the MDF (often by fiber). Typical floor plan: one IDF per floor.

RULE: Place IDFs so any user port is within 100 meters of copper cable from the IDF.

Racks & Rack Units

A rack unit (U) is 1.75 inches (44.45 mm) of vertical mounting space. Equipment is sized in U: a 1U switch occupies one slot, a 2U server occupies two, a 4U storage array occupies four. Common rack heights:

Power — Watts vs VA

This is the most-confused topic in 2.4. Memorize:

  • Volt-Amperes (VA) = "apparent power" — the product of voltage and current. UPSes and circuit breakers are rated in VA.
  • Watts (W) = "real power" — the actual energy being consumed. Devices are rated in watts.
  • Power Factor (PF) relates them: Watts = VA × PF. For modern IT equipment, PF is typically 0.9 to 0.95. Older equipment with non-PFC power supplies might be 0.7-0.8.
  • Practical impact: A 1500 VA UPS at PF 0.9 only delivers 1350W of real power. You can't load 1500W of devices onto it.
Circuit Sizing & the NEC 80% Rule
SINGLE-PHASE

120 V — standard US wall

Common in small server rooms and office IDFs. 15A or 20A circuits. Lower density.

15A circuit: 120V × 15A = 1800 VA capacity. Safe continuous: 1440 VA.

20A circuit: 120V × 20A = 2400 VA capacity. Safe continuous: 1920 VA.

DOUBLE-VOLTAGE

208 V or 240 V — data center

Standard in data centers. Higher voltage = more power per circuit at the same amperage.

208V × 20A: 4160 VA capacity. Safe continuous: 3328 VA.

208V × 30A: 6240 VA capacity. Safe continuous: 4992 VA.

THE 80% RULE

Continuous-load derating

The National Electrical Code (NEC, NFPA 70) requires continuous loads (running ≥ 3 hours) to be limited to 80% of the circuit rating. A 20A breaker handles 20A briefly but only 16A continuously.

WHY: Heat dissipation in breakers and wiring under sustained load.

PDU

Power Distribution Unit

The "fancy power strip" in a rack. Distributes power from one input (UPS output or wall) to multiple outlets. Models range from basic (just outlets) to switched (remotely toggle each outlet) and metered (per-outlet wattage reporting).

UPS — Uninterruptible Power Supply

A UPS provides three benefits:

  • Runtime during outages — battery backup keeps gear running long enough to fail over to a generator or gracefully shut down.
  • Power conditioning — filters spikes, sags, brownouts, and electrical noise.
  • Surge suppression — protects against lightning-induced and grid transient surges.

Environmental Controls
TEMPERATURE

ASHRAE recommended: 64-81°F (18-27°C)

The ASHRAE TC 9.9 thermal guidelines define the recommended envelope for IT equipment. Above 81°F: thermal throttling, accelerated component aging. Below 64°F: condensation risk when warm air re-enters, energy waste.

DESIGN: Hot-aisle/cold-aisle layout — server intakes face cold supply air, exhausts face hot return air.

HUMIDITY

Recommended: 40-60% RH

Too dry (<30%): Electrostatic discharge (ESD) — a 3500V spark can damage components silently. Carpet and synthetic clothing exacerbate this.

Too humid (>70%): Condensation, corrosion of contacts and connectors, mold.

AIRFLOW

Hot aisle / cold aisle

Rows of racks alternate orientation so fronts (intakes) face each other across a "cold aisle" and backs (exhausts) face each other across a "hot aisle." Prevents hot exhaust from being re-ingested by another rack.

UPGRADE: Containment — physical curtains or ceilings sealing hot or cold aisles.

FIRE SUPPRESSION

Clean agent for data centers

Wet-pipe sprinklers — cheap but water destroys gear. Bad fit for DC.
Pre-action (dry-pipe) — water releases only after fire detected (two-step). Better.
Clean agent (FM-200, Novec 1230, FE-13) — gaseous, leaves no residue, safe for people. Industry standard for data centers.
Inert gas (Inergen, Argonite) — displaces O₂ but maintains breathable level.
CO₂ — effective but kills people. Mostly deprecated.

Cabling — Plenum vs Riser
PLENUM (CMP)

Air-handling spaces

Cable jackets made of low-smoke, low-toxicity material. Required in plenum spaces — the air-return paths above ceilings and below raised floors that share air with the rest of the building.

WHY: If non-plenum cable burns, toxic smoke spreads through HVAC to every room.

RISER (CMR)

Between floors

Cable rated for vertical runs between floors. Less stringent than plenum but designed to resist flame spreading floor-to-floor.

RULE: Plenum-rated cable can substitute for riser, but riser cannot substitute for plenum.

Common Exam Traps
  • 1 U = 1.75 inches. Not 1.5, not 2. Exam testers love trick units.
  • VA × Power Factor = Watts. A 1500 VA UPS at PF 0.9 delivers 1350W. Don't size by VA alone.
  • NEC 80% rule applies to continuous loads. A 20A circuit's safe continuous capacity is 16A (1920 VA at 120V).
  • 208V/240V is data-center standard. 120V is for offices and small IDFs.
  • ASHRAE recommended temp is 64-81°F. The upper bound was raised over the years — older sources say 68-77°F.
  • Low humidity = static electricity. High humidity = corrosion. Both bad; both cause silent damage.
  • Clean agents (FM-200, Novec 1230) are the right answer for data center fire suppression. Not water, not CO₂.
  • Plenum-rated > riser-rated > general-purpose. Plenum can sub for riser; riser cannot sub for plenum.

How to read this

  • Green (≤60%) means safe. Plenty of headroom for growth and inrush surges.
  • Gold (60-80%) means warning. Approaching capacity. Plan for redundancy or upsize before adding more.
  • Red (>80%) means trouble. Either you're past NEC code (continuous loads must stay ≤ 80%) or your UPS is overcommitted.
  • The 80% line on each bar marks the NEC safe-continuous threshold. Cross it and you risk breaker trips, code violations, or UPS shutdown.
  • Power and rack space are independent. A heavily-loaded but mostly-empty rack is fine; a packed but lightly-loaded rack is also fine. Both at once is when you have a problem.

Why these ranges

  • Why not run cold (under 64°F)? Risk of condensation when warm room air mixes with cold cabinet air. Also wastes cooling energy — every degree of unnecessary cooling adds operating cost.
  • Why not run hot (over 81°F)? Component lifetimes shrink rapidly with temperature. Most electronics rate for 25°C operating; for every 10°C increase, failure rates roughly double (Arrhenius-type rule of thumb).
  • Why not too dry (under 40%)? Electrostatic discharge — a barely-noticed spark can be 3500V+ and silently damage components. Carpet and synthetic clothing make this worse.
  • Why not too humid (over 60%)? Corrosion on contacts and PCB traces. Above 70%, condensation forms on cool surfaces. Mold becomes a long-term risk.
  • Why clean agent over water? Water suppresses fire effectively but instantly ruins every powered device it touches. Clean agents (heptafluoropropane, fluoroketone) absorb heat and break the combustion chain reaction without conducting electricity or leaving residue.
